Designed and evaluated five convolutional neural networks of increasing depth to classify four stages of Alzheimer's disease from MRI scans: Non-Demented, Very Mild Demented, Mild Demented and Moderate Demented. The study identifies an optimal depth at 7 layers — both shallower and deeper models suffer severe overfitting and collapse to predicting a single class, making them clinically unusable.

Literature models use pretrained weights or multimodal data; this study built all architectures from scratch.

Optimal depth is non-monotonic. Performance peaks at 7 layers then degrades — additional layers introduce gradient instability without learning gain.

Training accuracy is a misleading signal. All 5 models reach 95–99% train accuracy; only the 7-layer model generalises. Validation monitoring is essential.

Prediction collapse is a clinical failure mode. Four of five models classify most inputs as class 2, rendering them unusable in a medical context.

Next steps: Transfer learning (ResNet50, InceptionV3), learning rate scheduling (ReduceLROnPlateau), 128×128 resolution, Grad-CAM visualisations.

End-to-end binary classification pipeline predicting whether passengers were transported to an alternative dimension. Four models trained across two algorithms (XGBoost, Random Forest) and two missing-value strategies (Simple, KNN imputation), with 3-fold cross-validated hyperparameter tuning. SHAP analysis reveals CryoSleep as the dominant predictor — over 6× more important than any other feature.

Simple Imputation

  • Categorical columns → filled with mode
  • Numerical columns → filled with median
  • Applied before encoding — fast, robust baseline
  • Best result: Kaggle 0.80383 (XGBoost)

KNN Imputation

  • Encode all categorical columns first
  • Mark unknowns with −1 sentinel value
  • Run KNN Imputer on encoded data
  • Re-apply encoding post-imputation

Key Findings & Next Steps

CryoSleep is the dominant predictor. SHAP analysis confirms it across both XGBoost models — passengers in cryo sleep spend nothing and are disproportionately transported, creating a near-deterministic signal.

Simple imputation outperformed KNN for XGBoost (0.80383 vs 0.79915). KNN introduced noise by imputing values that misaligned with the original distribution. For Random Forest, KNN marginally helped (0.80102 vs 0.79705).

Next steps: MICE / Iterative Imputation with domain logic (CryoSleep=True → all spending=0), LightGBM, CatBoost, stacking ensemble, Bayesian hyperparameter search, interaction features from PassengerId group structure.
